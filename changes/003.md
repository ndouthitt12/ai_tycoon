## 1. Shift to "Value-to-Price" (ROI) Ratios
Currently, the game calculates a baseAppeal (capability + goal alignment) and then simply subtracts a pricePenalty based on how far the price deviates from $18.00. This means a highly intelligent model priced at $30 suffers the same exact point penalty as a dumb model priced at $30.

Recommendation: Cohorts evaluate subscriptions based on whether the capability justifies the price.

Implementation: Instead of subtraction, use a ratio. Define an "Expected Capability per Dollar" for each cohort.

If a model has 80 Capability and costs $20, its ratio is 4.0. If a competitor has 50 Capability and costs $10, its ratio is 5.0.

A "Casual" cohort will heavily favor the 5.0 ratio (they want cheap, "good enough" AI). A "Developer" or "Law" cohort will ignore the ratio and focus on raw capability, accepting the $20 or even $40 price tag if the reasoning score is high enough.

## 2. Implement Psychological Budgets and Price Cliffs
The current math uses priceDelta * 15 * cohort.priceSensitivity to create a smooth, linear slope of lost appeal as prices rise. Real consumer psychology revolves around hard budget cliffs (e.g., "I will never pay more than $20 a month for a chatbot").

Recommendation: Give each cohort a maxBudget in their CohortDef.

Casual Users: maxBudget: 15. If the cheapest plan is $20, their appeal should instantly drop by 80% (an exponential cliff, not a linear slope).

Power Users/Developers: maxBudget: 40. They will gladly pay $20, $25, or $30 with very little friction, but charging $50 triggers the cliff.

## 3. Free Tier as an Anchor, Not Just Another Plan
Currently, the game groups free tiers and paid tiers into the same softmax weighting pool if multiple plans fit a cohort's needs.

Recommendation: Cohorts should explicitly evaluate the "Free to Paid Upgrade Delta."

When evaluating the product, the cohort first checks the Free Tier. If the Free Tier covers 80% of their token needs and has adequate capability, the probability of them choosing the Pro tier drops significantly.

The Pro plan's appeal should only be calculated based on the surplus value it offers over the Free plan (e.g., removing rate-limit friction, granting access to a smarter model, or guaranteeing uptime reliability). If the Free plan and Pro plan use the same model and the user doesn't hit the token cap, the Pro plan's appeal to that user should be exactly zero.
